## What a two weeks notice actually is

A two weeks notice is the short letter or email you give your employer to say you are leaving and to tell them your last day. In the United States, two weeks is the customary amount of notice for most jobs — long enough to be courteous and let your employer plan a handover, short enough not to drag out a departure. It is the document that closes one chapter of your career cleanly and protects the reference and relationships you will rely on in the next one.

The crucial thing to understand up front is that, in the US, two weeks notice is a courtesy, not a legal requirement. The default rule in almost every US state is at-will employment: either you or your employer can end the relationship at any time, with or without notice, for almost any lawful reason. You give two weeks because the professional world expects it and because burning the bridge costs you references, rehire eligibility, and reputation in your industry — not because the law compels it. The exceptions are an employment contract, a collective bargaining agreement, or a written company policy that specifies a longer or different period, all of which override the two-week default.

This is also where the US and UK diverge sharply. In the UK, there is no two-week convention — your notice period is whatever your employment contract says, commonly one month and often longer for senior roles, with a statutory minimum of one week once you have been employed for a month. UK readers should follow their contract and use the resignation letter template rather than the US two-week courtesy described here.

## When you need one

**Leaving a job on good terms.** The overwhelmingly common case. You have accepted a new role, or are leaving for personal reasons, and you want to depart professionally. Two weeks notice is the expected, reputation-preserving way to do it.

**Standard professional and office roles.** In most salaried US jobs, two weeks is the floor of acceptable notice. Giving less reads as abrupt; giving none can permanently damage a reference and is remembered in tight-knit industries.

**Hourly and shift work.** Two weeks notice is still the courtesy standard, though enforcement of any notice norm is looser. A written notice is worth giving even in hourly roles because it documents that you left properly, which matters if you ever reapply or need the manager as a reference.

**When you want a clean reference.** The notice period is your last impression. A professional notice and a helpful handover are what your former manager will remember when a future employer calls. The two weeks notice is, in effect, an investment in your next reference.

**When you do NOT need this template:** if you are in the UK or any market with contractual notice periods, if your contract specifies a notice period longer than two weeks, or if you are being asked to resign with immediate effect. In those cases, use the fuller resignation letter, which handles contractual and longer notice.

## What it must include

A two weeks notice is short, but four elements are non-negotiable:

**1. A clear statement that you are resigning.** No ambiguity. "I am writing to give two weeks notice of my resignation from my position as [title]." The reader should know in the first sentence exactly what the letter is.

**2. Your specific last working day.** This is where most notices go wrong. Do not write "in two weeks" — write the actual date. Count fourteen calendar days from your notice date and confirm it lands on, or adjust it to, your final scheduled working day.

**3. A professional, neutral tone.** Whatever your reasons, the notice is factual and calm. You do not need to explain why you are leaving; a brief positive note is enough.

**4. A sign-off and your name.** Close like any business letter. If sending by email, your name and contact details below the sign-off are sufficient.

Optional but recommended: a one-line thank-you and an offer to help with the transition. These two sentences cost nothing and materially improve how you are remembered.

## Variants you will encounter

**Standard letter.** The full version: your details, the date, your manager and company, a clear resignation statement with the last working day, a thank-you, a transition offer, and a sign-off. Printed and signed, or attached to an email. Best for professional and salaried roles.

**Email version.** The same content, formatted as an email with a clear subject line ("Resignation — [Your Name]") and no postal address block. Standard for remote teams and increasingly the default everywhere. Time-stamped and easy for HR to file. The builder produces an email-ready version when you select that delivery method.

**Brief / minimal.** Two or three sentences: you are resigning, your last day, thank you. Entirely acceptable, and the safest choice when you are leaving on difficult terms or simply prefer not to elaborate. A short neutral notice can never be used against you.

**US courtesy vs UK contractual.** The defining variant. The US two-week notice is a voluntary courtesy under at-will employment. The UK equivalent is governed by your contract and is a legal obligation — typically a month or more. If you are in the UK, do not use a two-week notice; follow your contract and the resignation letter template.

## Step-by-step

**Step 1 — Tell your manager first, if you can.** Where practical, have a short, calm conversation with your direct manager before the written notice arrives. It lets you control the message and keeps the relationship warm.

**Step 2 — State the resignation and the date.** Open with a clear resignation statement and your specific last working day. Count fourteen calendar days from your notice date and adjust to your final scheduled shift.

**Step 3 — Add a brief thank-you (optional).** One or two sentences thanking the employer for the opportunity. Keep it genuine and short. No need to explain your reasons.

**Step 4 — Offer to help with the handover.** A single sentence offering to wrap up projects, document your work, or help train a replacement. This protects your reference more than anything else in the letter.

**Step 5 — Sign off and deliver.** Close professionally with your name. Decide whether to send a printed letter, an email, or both, and copy HR so the resignation is officially logged.

<aside class="tip">
**Expert tip:** The single most common error in a two weeks notice is botching the date. People write "I am giving two weeks notice" and then either leave the last day vague or miscount it — handing in notice on a Friday and assuming the following Friday is "two weeks" when it is only one. Two weeks means fourteen calendar days. Open a calendar, count fourteen days from the date you will actually deliver the notice, land on the date, and check it falls on a working day for you; if it lands on a weekend or your day off, use the last scheduled working day before it. Then write that exact date in the letter — "My last working day will be Friday, June 20" — not "in two weeks." This removes any argument about when your employment ends, which matters for final pay, benefits, and your last paycheck.
</aside>

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Miscounting the fourteen-day date.** The most frequent error. People misjudge "two weeks," leave the date vague, or forget that the last working day should be a day they actually work. Count fourteen calendar days, adjust to your final scheduled shift, and write the specific date.

**Mistake 2: Treating two weeks as legally required.** In the US it is a courtesy under at-will employment, not a legal obligation (absent a contract or policy). Conversely, assuming the US two-week norm applies in the UK is wrong — UK notice is contractual. Know which regime you are in.

**Mistake 3: Over-explaining the reason for leaving.** A notice is a notification, not a justification. Listing grievances, criticising colleagues, or writing a detailed account of why you are unhappy adds risk and no benefit. Keep it neutral and brief; raise concerns in an exit interview if at all.

**Mistake 4: Skipping the in-person conversation.** Sending a cold written notice that your manager reads before you have spoken to them damages a relationship you may need later. Talk first where you can, then put it in writing the same day.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting the transition offer.** Leaving without offering to hand over your work makes the departure harder for the team and is remembered. One sentence offering to help is the cheapest reputation insurance available.

## Worked example

Maria Delgado is a marketing coordinator at a Chicago software company. She has accepted a new role starting in three weeks and wants to leave her current job cleanly.

On Monday June 2, she has a short call with her manager, Tom, to tell him she is resigning. The same day she sends a two weeks notice by email with the subject line "Resignation — Maria Delgado." She counts fourteen calendar days from June 2, landing on June 16, a Monday — her final scheduled working day.

Her notice reads: "I am writing to give two weeks notice of my resignation from my position as Marketing Coordinator at Riverline Software. My last working day will be Monday, June 16, 2026. I am grateful for the past two years and everything I have learned on the team. I am happy to document my current projects and help train whoever takes over the campaign calendar during my remaining time." She signs off "Sincerely, Maria Delgado" with her phone number below.

The notice is clear, dated to a specific working day, gracious, and offers a handover. Tom forwards it to HR, who confirm her final pay and accrued PTO payout. Maria leaves on good terms and lists Tom as a reference for her next move.

## Protecting the relationship on your way out

The temptation when leaving a job — especially one you are glad to be leaving — is to treat the resignation as the end of a relationship that no longer matters. This is almost always a mistake, and understanding why reframes how you should write and deliver your notice. The professional world is smaller and more connected than it appears. Your current manager may move to a company you later want to work for; your colleagues become a network you will draw on for years; and, most concretely, the people you are leaving are the people a future employer will call when they check your references. The two weeks notice and the handover that follows it are, in effect, the last and most lasting impression you leave, and that impression attaches to your name long after you have gone.

This is why the small courtesies pay off so far out of proportion to their cost. A calm conversation with your manager before the written notice arrives, a neutral and gracious tone in the letter regardless of how you feel, a genuine offer to document your work and help train a replacement, and a clean, complete handover during the notice period — none of these take much effort, and together they are what your former colleagues will remember. The person who leaves well is remembered as a professional; the person who coasts through their notice, or vents on the way out, is remembered as someone to be wary of. In a tight industry, that memory follows you.

The handover itself deserves real attention, because it is where the goodwill is actually earned or lost. Use the notice period to write down what you know that no one else does — the passwords and access, the half-finished projects and their status, the relationships and the context a successor will need, the recurring tasks and their deadlines. A departing employee who leaves behind a clear handover document does their team a genuine service and is remembered fondly for it; one who walks out leaving chaos behind is remembered the other way. The fourteen days of a US notice period are short, but they are enough to leave things in good order, and doing so is the single most effective way to protect the references and relationships you will rely on next.
