Education templates are a category where the design of the template is itself the educational intervention. The Cornell notes format does not just organise information — the specific structure of the note-taking page is designed to trigger retrieval practice and active review, which are among the most evidence-backed learning strategies in cognitive science. A Venn diagram does not just display overlapping information — the act of placing items in the correct zone forces categorical analysis. The form and the function are inseparable.

This is worth stating because a lot of education templates are designed by graphic designers who have optimised for visual appeal rather than learning efficacy. A note-taking template that looks beautiful but puts the recall column in the wrong place misses the point entirely. The templates in this cluster are built around the pedagogical intent of the underlying methods, not just the visual format.

The cluster serves two main audiences: students who are organising their own learning, and teachers who are designing classroom activities and materials. The templates that serve both (Cornell notes, Venn diagrams, graphic organisers) are optimised for student use; those more teacher-specific (Jeopardy, All About Me) assume a classroom context. Both UK and US educational conventions are addressed where they differ.

## What this category covers

**Cornell notes.** A note-taking format developed by Walter Pauk at Cornell University in the 1950s, originally published in *How to Study in College* (first edition 1962, now in its tenth edition). The page is divided into three sections: a narrow recall column on the left (approximately one-third of page width), a wider notes column on the right (two-thirds), and a summary section at the bottom. The intended use: during a lecture or reading, notes go in the right column; within 24 hours, key questions and cues go in the left column (recall prompts, not more notes); the summary at the bottom is written after reviewing both columns. The method's effectiveness comes from the forced retrieval practice when you cover the notes column and try to answer the questions in the recall column — a technique with strong support in the cognitive psychology literature on spaced repetition and the testing effect (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006).

The template pre-marks the column widths and section boundaries. The most common mistake in self-made Cornell notes is making the recall column too wide, which encourages note-taking there rather than question-writing.

**Venn diagram.** Two or more overlapping circles, each representing a set or category. Items that belong to only one set go in the non-overlapping segment of that circle; items that belong to both go in the intersection. The Venn diagram was formalised by the logician John Venn in 1880 (published in *Philosophical Magazine*). It is worth noting a distinction that matters in academic and professional contexts: a Venn diagram shows logical set membership (any item can in principle be in any combination of sets); an Euler diagram shows only the relationships that actually exist in the data, omitting intersections that are empty. Many documents labelled "Venn diagram" are technically Euler diagrams. For classroom use, the distinction is often irrelevant; for data visualisation in formal presentations, it matters.

**Graphic organiser.** A visual framework for organising information — a category that includes Venn diagrams but extends to mind maps, concept maps, KWL charts (Know/Want to Know/Learned), story maps, sequence chains, and cause-and-effect diagrams. Graphic organisers have a substantial evidence base in educational research: Marzano's *Classroom Instruction That Works* (2001) identifies nonlinguistic representation (of which graphic organisers are the primary example) as one of the nine instructional strategies with the highest effect sizes on student achievement. The template here provides a library of the most commonly used graphic organiser types, each with the fields appropriate to its specific thinking structure.

**Jeopardy template.** A PowerPoint or Google Slides-compatible template replicating the format of the US game show *Jeopardy!* — a grid of categories and point values where questions are revealed behind each cell. Used by teachers across age groups and subjects as a review game. The template provides a 5-category × 5-point-value grid (typically 100, 200, 300, 400, 500 points) with reveal mechanics built into the slide transitions. UK teachers use this format despite the show being less prominent in UK culture; the format is immediately intuitive regardless of whether students have seen the original show.

The name "Jeopardy" and the show's specific trade dress are registered trademarks of Sony Pictures Television. The game format itself — asking players to formulate a question for a given answer — is not protectable. Educational use templates that use a similar grid format without replicating the show's visual branding (the blue background, the specific Jeopardy! logo, the dollar-sign currency) are standard classroom practice and fall within educational fair use in the US and fair dealing in the UK.

**Avery 5160 label template.** Avery 5160 is a specific label sheet format: 30 labels per sheet, each label 1 inch × 2⅝ inches (25.4 mm × 66.7 mm), arranged in a 3-column × 10-row grid on an 8.5" × 11" sheet. It is the most widely sold address label format in the US; the Avery 5160 code has become effectively a generic term for this label size, even when used with non-Avery brand sheets. The template provides an accurate 30-up grid pre-formatted for Microsoft Word and Google Docs. The most common use: printing mailing address labels, name tags for events, and classroom labels.

**Avery label (general).** The broader Avery label category extends beyond 5160 to the full Avery product range: 5161 (same size, slightly different spacing), 5163 (10 labels per sheet, larger 2" × 4" size for parcel labels), 5167 (80 labels per sheet for small labels), and others. The general Avery label template accommodates the most common sizes with selectable grid configurations.

**SOAP note.** A structured clinical documentation format used in healthcare settings: Subjective (patient's reported symptoms and complaints), Objective (measurable, observable findings — vital signs, exam results), Assessment (the clinician's diagnosis or differential), Plan (treatment, referral, follow-up). SOAP notes were developed by Lawrence Weed at the University of Vermont in the 1960s as part of the Problem-Oriented Medical Record system. In the UK, SOAP notes are used in primary care, mental health settings, and nursing documentation. In the US, they are standard across medical, dental, and allied health professions. The template here is used by healthcare students (nursing, physiotherapy, medical students practising documentation), not for clinical use where specific institutional documentation systems apply.

**All About Me.** A personal introduction template used in classrooms — primarily in primary/elementary school — to help students and teachers learn about each other at the start of a term or year. Standard sections: name, age, family, pets, hobbies, favourite subjects, aspirations. Used as an icebreaker activity and as a classroom display document. Also used by teachers as a back-to-school communication tool sent to parents. The format is universal across UK and US education, though the specific prompts vary by age group and cultural context.

## How to pick the right template

The clearest selection factor is: is this template for the student or for the teacher?

**Student-facing templates.** Cornell notes (for lecture/reading note-taking), Venn diagram (for comparison tasks), graphic organiser (for any structured thinking task), SOAP note (for healthcare students in clinical practice).

**Teacher-facing templates.** Jeopardy (for review activities), All About Me (for classroom community building), Avery labels (for classroom organisation and labelling).

**Both.** Most graphic organisers can be used by either students independently or distributed by teachers as structured worksheets.

**Age and level.** All About Me is primarily primary school (UK KS1–KS2; US grades K–5). Cornell notes are most effective from secondary school (UK KS3+; US grades 6+) onwards — the method requires metacognitive self-regulation that younger students are still developing. SOAP notes are post-16 or higher education only, specific to healthcare programmes.

**Worked example — GCSE student, England.** Tom is a Year 10 student (14 years old) studying for his GCSEs. He has just come back from a double-period history lesson on the causes of World War I and wants to consolidate his notes.

He uses the Cornell notes template. During the lesson, he wrote notes in the right column (Franz Ferdinand assassination, alliance system, naval race between Britain and Germany, Moroccan crises 1905 and 1911, mobilisation timetables). That evening, he writes recall questions in the left column: "What were the MAIN causes?" "Why did the alliance system make war more likely?" "Which country mobilised first?" He covers the right column and tries to answer from memory — finding he cannot answer the mobilisation question, which tells him exactly what to revisit.

The following week, the teacher announces a Jeopardy review game using the same content. The category "Alliance Systems" for 300 points reveals: "This country's alliance with Austria-Hungary meant it was obligated to enter a conflict it had not started." Tom knows the answer (Germany) because his Cornell notes recall column had that exact question.

This is the method working as designed: the retrieval practice in Cornell notes creates the memory that makes the Jeopardy game answerable.

**On Venn vs. Euler.** For teachers: when a student creates a Venn diagram and leaves the intersection blank because there are no items that belong to both categories, that is technically an Euler diagram, not a Venn diagram. This is worth knowing if you are discussing logic or set theory, but for most classroom comparison tasks (Compare the US to the UK; Compare photosynthesis to respiration), the distinction can be set aside. It becomes important in maths and statistics contexts where precision in notation matters.

## Common mistakes in this category

**Mistake 1: Cornell notes used as fancy two-column notes.** The most common misuse: students write notes in both columns, filling the recall column with additional notes rather than with retrieval cues. This defeats the purpose of the format. The recall column should contain questions, keywords, or prompts — not more content. If a student's recall column contains sentences and full explanations, they are writing Cornell-format notes rather than doing Cornell-method note-taking.

**Mistake 2: Graphic organisers given without instruction in how to use them.** Research on graphic organisers (Marzano, 2001; Hattie, 2009) shows positive effects on learning — but those effects are mediated by whether students understand how to use the tool. Handing a KWL chart to a class with no explanation produces random entries. The "K" column (what do you already know) frequently triggers guessing rather than genuine prior knowledge retrieval. Brief explicit instruction before each use is the difference between the tool working and not.

**Mistake 3: Jeopardy templates where all teams answer simultaneously.** The standard classroom Jeopardy implementation has all teams competing to buzz in first. The result: one or two fast teams dominate, and the rest disengage. A more effective implementation is "write it down and reveal simultaneously" — all teams write their answer, then reveal. This maximises engagement and assessment value. The template includes a note on this implementation variant.

**Mistake 4: Avery labels printed without checking bleed margins.** Printing directly to Avery label sheets without checking that the content fits within the label border produces labels with text cut off at the edges. The template has 1mm inset margins on all sides; do not reduce these. Also: print a test page on plain paper before using label sheets, and hold it up to the sheet to check alignment. Printer calibration varies; a half-millimetre offset across 10 rows adds up.

**Mistake 5: SOAP notes written without sufficient Objective section.** Healthcare students tend to write strong Subjective sections (patient story) and weak Objective sections (measurable findings). The Objective section should contain specific quantified data: blood pressure 138/86 mmHg, respiratory rate 18/min, Glasgow Coma Scale 15/15, BMI 27.3. Vague Objective entries ("patient appeared pale and tired") belong in the Subjective section, not the Objective. The distinction matters because the Assessment must be justified by the Objective findings, not by the clinician's impressionistic sense.

**Mistake 6: All About Me templates distributed to secondary/high school students.** All About Me is developmentally appropriate for primary/elementary age. Giving the same template to 14-year-olds will produce groans and reluctance; the questions feel infantilising. For secondary school icebreaker activities, a modified format with more sophisticated prompts ("A fact about you that surprises people", "The thing you're most looking forward to this year", "One question you want answered by the end of this year") achieves the same relationship-building purpose at an age-appropriate level.

<aside class="tip">
**Expert tip:** The summary section at the bottom of Cornell notes is the most frequently skipped part of the method and the most valuable. Writing a 3–5 sentence summary after completing the recall column — entirely from memory, without looking at the notes column — forces you to synthesise what you have learned into your own words. This is the encoding mechanism that moves information from short-term to long-term memory. Students who use Cornell notes and skip the summary are capturing about 60% of the method's benefit. If you can only do one additional step after the lecture, it is this one.
</aside>

## Closing

The highest-impact starting point in this cluster for most students is the Cornell notes template, because it introduces a retrieval-practice habit that pays dividends across every subject, not just the one you used it for first. The evidence base for the testing effect — using self-testing as a learning strategy rather than re-reading — is one of the most consistent findings in applied cognitive psychology, and the Cornell method is the lowest-effort implementation of that principle. If you are a teacher looking for a quick-win classroom tool, the Jeopardy template is the highest-engagement review activity that requires no special equipment or prior student preparation. For anyone dealing with label printing — classroom, office, or home — the Avery 5160 template saves the 20 minutes of margin adjustment that everyone wastes the first time they try to print to a label sheet.
